# Seborrheic Keratosis (Brown Warts, Basal Cell Papillomas)

## Overview

Seborrheic keratosis is a harmless, warty spot that appears in adulthood as a common sign of skin aging.

## Demographics

Over 90% of adults over 60 have one or more seborrheic keratoses. Lesions usually start to appear in the 30s or 40s and are uncommon under age 20. They occur in men and women of all races, and some people inherit a tendency to develop very large numbers.

## Causes

Seborrheic keratoses are considered age-related, not infectious — viral involvement seems unlikely. Eruptive lesions can follow sunburn or dermatitis, and skin friction may explain why they appear in body folds. Stable, clonal mutations have been identified in FGFR3, PIK3CA, RAS, AKT1, and EGFR; FGFR3 mutations are linked to increased age and head/neck location, hinting at a role for UV radiation. They don't carry tumor-suppressor gene mutations.

## Clinical features

Seborrheic keratoses look like they're stuck on to the skin — like barnacles. They appear as flat or raised papules or plaques ranging from 1 mm to several centimeters, with a smooth, waxy, or warty surface. Color varies from skin-colored, yellow, gray, light brown, dark brown, or black to mixed colors. They can appear anywhere except the palms and soles, occurring solitarily or grouped — within the scalp, under the breasts, over the spine, or in the groin.

Recognized subtypes include:

- **Solar lentigo** — flat pigmented patches in sun-exposed sites.
- **Dermatosis papulosa nigra** — small, pedunculated, heavily pigmented lesions on the head and neck (more common in darker skin).
- **Stucco keratoses** — gray, white, or yellow papules on the lower extremities.
- **Inverted follicular keratosis**.
- **Lichenoid keratosis** — an inflammatory phase before the lesion fades.

## Key distinguishing features

- **"Stuck-on" appearance** — *the surface looks pasted onto the skin like a barnacle, rather than continuous with the surrounding epidermis.*
- **Sharp, well-demarcated borders** — *unlike SCC in situ or BCC, which tend to have irregular or ill-defined edges.*
- **Pseudohorn cysts on dermoscopy** — *milia-like cysts and comedo-like openings producing a "brain-like" (cerebriform) pattern are characteristic.*
- **Adult-onset and stable** — *first appearance in the 30s–40s, with stable size and uniform color over time. Sudden change or rapid eruption warrants further evaluation.*
- **Spares palms and soles** — *helps differentiate from acral nevi and other palmoplantar lesions.*

## Variation in skin tones

**Dermatosis papulosa nigra** is a variant seen most often in patients with skin of color — small, pedunculated, heavily pigmented seborrheic keratoses on the head and neck. Treatment-induced loss of pigment is a particular concern in patients with darker skin tones, and clinicians should consider this when choosing destructive treatments.

## Diagnosis

Most seborrheic keratoses are diagnosed by inspection: a stuck-on, well-demarcated warty plaque, often alongside other similar lesions. Dermoscopy shows multiple orange or brown clods (from keratin in skin-surface crevices), white milia-like clods, and curved thick ridges and furrows forming a "brain-like" (cerebriform) pattern. Because seborrheic keratoses can resemble basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, or melanoma, a partial shave or punch biopsy — or diagnostic excision — is warranted when there's any doubt.

## Treatment

Seborrheic keratoses are removed when they're unsightly, itchy, or catch on clothing. Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen suits thinner lesions; curettage with or without electrocautery, ablative laser surgery, scalpel shave biopsy, and focal chemical peel with trichloracetic acid are alternatives. There's no easy way to remove many lesions in a single session.

## Risk factors and associations

Age and chronic sun exposure are the main risk factors, supported by genetic and epidemiological data; family history predicts the eventual lesion count. Several medications have been associated with eruptive seborrheic keratoses — adalimumab, vemurafenib, dabrafenib, 5-fluorouracil, and various chemotherapy drugs. Dermatitis can also trigger new lesions. **Leser-Trélat sign** — sudden eruption of multiple seborrheic keratoses — very rarely signals an underlying internal malignancy (most often gastric adenocarcinoma) and warrants further workup.
