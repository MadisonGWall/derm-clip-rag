# Verruca Vulgaris (Common Warts)

## Overview

Common warts (verruca vulgaris) appear as cauliflower-like papules with a rough, hyperkeratotic surface, ranging from 1 mm to 1 cm or more.

## Demographics

Common warts are particularly common in school-aged children, though they can occur at any age. Risk goes up in patients with dermatitis (a defective skin barrier) and in those who are immunosuppressed — including people on long-term azathioprine or ciclosporin and HIV-positive individuals.

## Causes

The HPV types most often infecting the skin are 1, 2, 3, 4, 10, 27, 29, and 57. HPV is spread by direct skin contact or autoinoculation: scratching or picking a wart can move virus to an area like under a fingernail (subungual wart) or to another site of skin. The incubation period can be as long as twelve months, depending on how much virus is inoculated.

## Clinical features

Cutaneous viral warts have a hard, keratin-rich surface. Tiny red or black dots visible inside the wart are papillary capillaries — small blood vessels that have either thrombosed or remain patent and bleed pinpoint when the lesion is pared down. Common warts are most often found on the knees, the backs of fingers and toes, and around the nails (periungual). They can be solitary or multiple.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Hyperkeratotic, papillomatous (cauliflower-like) surface** — *texture distinguishes warts from smooth lesions like nevi or epidermal cysts.*
- **Black dots from thrombosed capillaries** — *the classic dermoscopic and clinical clue; pseudohorn cysts (seen in seborrheic keratosis) are absent.*
- **Disrupted skin lines** — *normal skin markings stop abruptly at the wart edge, unlike calluses or seborrheic keratoses where lines often remain visible.*
- **Distribution on knees, hands, and around nails** — *contact-driven sites differ from sun-exposed lesions like BCC or SCC in situ.*
- **Pinpoint bleeding when pared** — *patent capillaries bleed at the surface, a feature absent in calluses or corns.*

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical. Pinpoint red or black dots — the papillary capillaries — appear when the wart is pared down, and patent capillaries bleed at the surface. Dermoscopy makes these capillaries easier to see and helps distinguish a wart from other warty growths like seborrheic keratosis. A skin biopsy is sometimes needed when squamous cell carcinoma can't be excluded clinically. The main lookalikes are seborrheic keratosis, squamous cell carcinoma, plantar corn, and callus.

## Treatment

Natural history is favorable: in children, even without treatment, 50% of warts disappear within six months and 90% are gone within 2 years. Warts persist longer in adults but usually clear eventually. First-line topical treatment is a wart paint, paste, or patch containing salicylic acid, podophyllin, or similar — about 70% of warts resolve within twelve weeks of daily application. Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen achieves a similar 70% success rate after 3–4 months of regular freezing. Second-line options include imiquimod (often ineffective for cutaneous warts), intralesional bleomycin, pulsed dye laser, photodynamic therapy, laser vaporization, contact sensitizers, microwave therapy, and topical methotrexate with microneedling. **No single treatment is universally effective.**

## Risk factors and associations

Major risk factors are immunosuppression (drug-induced or HIV) and dermatitis with a defective skin barrier. Occupational exposure can drive a specific variant: **butcher's warts**, caused by HPV 7, infect the hands of butchers and others whose work involves chronic exposure to a cold, moist environment. Tobacco smoking has been linked to higher recurrence after treatment.
