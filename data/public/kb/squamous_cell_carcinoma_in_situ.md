# Squamous Cell Carcinoma In Situ

## Overview

Squamous cell carcinoma in situ — also called intraepidermal squamous cell carcinoma or Bowen's disease — is a common superficial form of keratinocyte cancer. The malignant cells stay confined within the epidermis (above the basement membrane), so the lesion is dysplastic but hasn't invaded deeper tissue.

## Demographics

Squamous cell carcinoma in situ mainly affects people with chronically sun-exposed skin. Up to 50% of patients have other keratinocyte skin cancers — most often basal cell carcinoma. The DermNet page doesn't give specific age, sex, or ethnicity breakdowns, but the lesion is most common in older patients with lighter skin tones.

## Causes

UV radiation is the main cause. UV damages skin-cell DNA, producing mutant clones of the **p53 gene** that drive uncontrolled cell growth. Other contributors include human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, arsenic ingestion, ionizing radiation, and immunosuppression (from disease or medication).

## Clinical features

Squamous cell carcinoma in situ presents as one or more irregular scaly plaques up to several centimeters in diameter. They're often orange-red but can also be brown. Lesions are most often diagnosed on sun-exposed sites — the ears, face, hands, and lower legs.

When the nail unit is involved, the presentation can include **erythronychia** (a longitudinal red band on the nail) with potential nail destruction.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Persistent, slow-growing scaly plaque** — *unlike eczema or psoriasis, squamous cell carcinoma in situ doesn't respond to topical steroids and persists over months to years.*
- **Sharp, irregular borders** — *contrasts with the smooth, sharply demarcated borders of seborrheic keratosis and the rolled border of BCC.*
- **Orange-red or brown color** — *helps differentiate from the dull tan/brown of SK and the pearly translucence of BCC.*
- **Sun-exposed distribution (ears, face, hands, lower legs)** — *photoaged context favors keratinocyte cancer over benign mimics.*
- **Glomerular (coiled) vessels on dermoscopy** — *crops of rounded, coiled blood vessels are supportive findings.*

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis starts clinically and dermoscopically: a red, scaly, irregular plaque is suspicious if dermoscopy reveals crops of rounded, coiled (glomerular) blood vessels. Biopsy confirms the diagnosis — histology shows full-thickness dysplasia of the epidermis confined above the basement membrane.

## Treatment

There are several first-line options, with the choice often guided by lesion site and size:

- **Topical 5-fluorouracil** — four-week course; combination products with calcipotriol have shown favorable response rates.
- **Imiquimod cream** — applied 3–5 times weekly for 4–16 weeks.
- **Photodynamic therapy** — methyl-levulinate cream PDT gives high cure rates for lesions on the face or lower legs.
- **Cryotherapy**, **curettage with electrosurgery**, **surgical excision**, and **observation** are additional options depending on size, site, and patient factors.

## Risk factors and associations

The main risk factors are chronic UV exposure and lighter skin tones, with additional contributions from HPV infection, arsenic ingestion, ionizing radiation, and immunosuppression. **Invasive SCC arises in about 5% of squamous cell carcinoma in situ lesions.** Recurrence after treatment is common — particularly in immunosuppressed patients — and treated patients face increased risk of new SCC lesions and other skin cancers (including BCC and melanoma).
