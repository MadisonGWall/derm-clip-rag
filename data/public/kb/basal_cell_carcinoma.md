# Basal Cell Carcinoma

## Overview

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is a common, locally invasive keratinocyte cancer (a non-melanoma skin cancer) and is the most common skin cancer overall. Metastasis is exceedingly rare except in neglected or aggressive cases.

## Demographics

BCC mostly affects elderly men, but it occurs across ages and sexes. Patients with lighter skin tones (often with blue eyes and blonde or red hair) face the highest risk; however, BCC can also affect darker skin tones. Patients with a history of prior skin cancer or significant sun damage have higher incidence, and many develop multiple primary tumors over time.

## Causes

Most BCCs involve DNA mutations in the **PTCH (patched) tumor-suppressor gene** within the **hedgehog signaling pathway**, often triggered by UV radiation. Other contributors include ionizing radiation, arsenic exposure, immunosuppression (from disease or medication), and certain medications such as hydrochlorothiazide. Both spontaneous and inherited gene defects predispose to BCC.

## Clinical features

BCC presents as a slow-growing plaque or nodule that's skin-colored, pink, or pigmented, ranging from a few millimeters to several centimeters. Spontaneous bleeding or ulceration may occur.

The recognized subtypes are:

- **Nodular BCC** — the most common type on the face: a shiny, pearly nodule with rolled edges and surface arborizing telangiectasias (branching blood vessels).
- **Superficial BCC** — common in younger adults; thin, translucent borders with small surface erosions.
- **Morpheaform (sclerosing) BCC** — waxy and scar-like, with wide and deep extension that isn't visible on the surface.
- **Basosquamous BCC** — a mixed BCC–SCC variant that can behave more aggressively.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Pearly, translucent surface** — *the hallmark sheen distinguishes BCC from dull, waxy seborrheic keratoses and from rough, hyperkeratotic warts.*
- **Arborizing telangiectasias** — *fine branching vessels visible on the surface or by dermoscopy; absent in nevi, SK, and verruca.*
- **Rolled border** — *a smooth, raised edge surrounding the lesion; differs from the irregular flat plaque of SCC in situ.*
- **Central ulceration ("rodent ulcer")** — *seen in nodular BCC and helps differentiate from non-malignant raised papules.*
- **Sun-exposed distribution** — *face, head/neck, and upper trunk; chronic photoaging context favors BCC over benign lookalikes.*

## Variation in skin tones

BCC is most common in patients with lighter skin tones but does occur in darker skin tones. **Pigmented BCC** is more frequent in skin of color and can mimic a benign nevus or seborrheic keratosis on first inspection. DermNet maintains a separate page on BCC in skin of color for further detail.

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis starts clinically — the typical pearly papule with telangiectasias and a rolled border. Dermoscopy adds detail: arborizing vessels, ulceration, blue-gray ovoid nests, and leaf-like areas. The diagnosis and histologic subtype are usually confirmed by biopsy or after excision. Non-invasive optical coherence tomography (a kind of light-based skin imaging) is increasingly used as an adjunct.

## Treatment

For primary BCC, **excision** with a 3–5 mm margin is the standard. **Mohs micrographic surgery** — staged excision with margin checking under the microscope — gives very high cure rates and is preferred for high-risk facial sites. **Curettage and electrocautery** suits small lesions; **cryotherapy** with liquid nitrogen suits small superficial BCCs. **Topical** treatments — imiquimod and 5-fluorouracil — are options for superficial lesions under 2 cm, and **photodynamic therapy** offers excellent cosmetic results for low-risk superficial disease. **Radiotherapy** is used when surgery isn't suitable. For advanced or metastatic BCC, treatment combines surgery, radiotherapy, and **hedgehog pathway inhibitors** (vismodegib, sonidegib).

## Risk factors and associations

Risk factors include older age, male sex, prior BCC or other skin cancer, photoaging, sunburn history, lighter skin tones (blue eyes, blonde or red hair), cutaneous injury or thermal burn, ionizing radiation, arsenic exposure, immunosuppression, and certain medications. Inherited syndromes that strongly predispose to BCC include **Gorlin syndrome** (basal cell nevus syndrome), Bazex-Dupré-Christol syndrome, Rombo syndrome, Oley syndrome, and **xeroderma pigmentosum**. Most BCCs are cured by treatment, but about 50% of patients develop a second BCC within 3 years and remain at elevated risk for other skin cancers including melanoma — long-term annual skin checks are warranted.
