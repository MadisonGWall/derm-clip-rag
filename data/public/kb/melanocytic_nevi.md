# Melanocytic Nevi (Moles)

## Overview

A melanocytic nevus, or mole, is a common benign skin lesion caused by a local cluster of pigment cells (melanocytes).

## Demographics

Almost everyone has at least one mole. About 1% of people are born with one or more congenital nevi. People with lighter skin tones tend to have more nevi than those with skin of color — averaging around 20–50 in the lightest skin tones. Most lesions appear in childhood (ages 2–10), and nevi tend to run in families: people with many nevi often have relatives with similar lesions.

## Causes

Why nevus cells cluster locally isn't fully understood, but the number a person develops depends on genetic factors, sun exposure, and immune status. Mutations in RAS genes are linked to congenital nevi. New nevi can erupt in patients on BRAF inhibitor drugs like vemurafenib or dabrafenib, and immunosuppressive treatment increases nevus counts.

## Clinical features

Nevi can appear anywhere on the body and look different depending on the site. They may be flat or raised, ranging in color from pink or flesh tones to dark brown, steel blue, or black. Sizes range from a few millimeters to several centimeters. Most are round or oval, though unusual shapes occur.

Nevi are classified by where the nevus cells sit in the skin:

- **Junctional nevus** — flat, with cells at the junction of the epidermis and dermis.
- **Dermal nevus** — a papule or nodule, with cells in the dermis.
- **Compound nevus** — a central raised area with a flat surrounding patch.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Symmetry and uniformity** — *most benign nevi are symmetric and uniformly colored, in contrast to melanoma's irregular borders and color variation.*
- **Stability over time** — *benign nevi remain stable; change in size, shape, or color is the strongest signal for biopsy.*
- **"Ugly duckling" sign** — *a nevus that looks meaningfully different from the patient's other nevi is more likely to be atypical or melanoma.*
- **Onset in childhood (ages 2–10)** — *new nevi developing after age 40 should raise suspicion for atypical lesions or melanoma.*
- **ABCD criteria absent** — *Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, and Diameter >6 mm should be absent in benign nevi; their presence prompts further evaluation.*

## Variation in skin tones

People with lighter skin tones tend to have lighter-colored nevi, while people with skin of color tend to have dark brown or black nevi. Site-specific dermoscopy patterns also vary: facial nevi show a "pseudonetwork" of pigment around hair follicles; nevi on the palms and soles (acral nevi) tend to show parallel lines; and nail-unit nevi cause a uniform longitudinal pigmented band on the nail. Acral and nail-unit lesions need careful evaluation in skin of color, since acral melanoma is also more common in this population.

## Diagnosis

Most nevi are diagnosed by inspection. Refer to a dermatologist if a nevus changes in size, shape, structure, or color; a new nevus appears after age 40; the lesion looks different from the patient's other nevi ("ugly duckling"); ABCD criteria are present; or there's bleeding, crusting, or itching. Dermoscopy helps recognize patterns (reticular, globular, starburst), and digital dermoscopic imaging supports follow-up of flat lesions with questionable features. **Partial biopsy is not recommended** — it may miss the area of cancerous change. Suspicious lesions warrant full excision biopsy.

## Treatment

Nevi are removed to rule out cancer, when irritated by clothing or grooming, or for cosmetic reasons. Excision biopsy is used for flat or suspicious lesions; shave biopsy for protruding nevi. Electrosurgery and laser are alternatives for cosmetic removal or to reduce coarse hair, though laser doesn't provide tissue for histology.

## Risk factors and associations

People with many nevi have a higher melanoma risk, especially those with over 100 lesions. **Atypical (dysplastic) nevi** — large (>5 mm), with ill-defined borders, color variation, and a mix of flat and bumpy components — carry additional melanoma risk and may meet specific histology criteria. Sun exposure starting in childhood is the dominant modifiable risk factor; sunscreen alone isn't enough to prevent new nevi from appearing. Recognized variants include **Meyerson nevus** (itchy, surrounded by eczema), **halo nevus** (white halo that gradually fades over years), and **recurrent nevus** (pigment that reappears in a scar after surgical removal).
