# Mycosis Fungoides

## Overview

Mycosis fungoides is the most common type of primary cutaneous T-cell lymphoma — a non-Hodgkin lymphoma of skin-homing CD4+ T cells. Clinically it tends to progress through three stages over time: patches, then plaques, then tumors. On biopsy, the malignant T cells migrate into the outer skin layer (epidermis) — a feature called epidermotropism that helps confirm the diagnosis.

## Demographics

Mycosis fungoides is rare overall — about 6.4 cases per million worldwide — but it accounts for roughly half of all primary cutaneous lymphomas. Median age at onset is 55–60 years, with a 2:1 male predominance in patients with lighter skin tones. Importantly, when patients are diagnosed under age 30, they're more often patients with skin of color — particularly Asian, Pacific Islander, or of African descent.

## Causes

The cause of mycosis fungoides isn't known. Leading theories include long-term immune stimulation by an unknown antigen, an inherited tendency (linked to certain HLA types), or chronic chemical exposure (some medications have been suggested). Cases sometimes cluster geographically and within families, hinting at environmental triggers — but unlike some other forms of cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, no virus has been linked to it.

## Clinical features

Mycosis fungoides progresses through three clinical stages, and lesions can stay in one stage for years:

- **Patch stage** — poorly defined, finely scaling red patches on sun-protected skin, often in a "bathing-suit" distribution (lower trunk, thighs, breasts). Patches are often asymptomatic. A **hypopigmented variant** appears as pale, finely scaly patches without skin thinning, and is more common in skin of color and in children.
- **Plaque stage** — better-defined, ring-shaped or curved (annular/arciform), thickened patches that itch. Color ranges from red to violaceous to brown. Initially these cover less than 10% of the body's surface.
- **Tumor stage** — larger irregular lumps (>1 cm) growing out of plaques, deep red to violaceous in color.

Recognized variants include **folliculotropic mycosis fungoides** (lymphoma cells track around hair follicles), **pagetoid reticulosis**, **granulomatous slack skin**, and a palms-and-soles variant called **mycosis fungoides palmaris et plantaris**.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Persistent patches in sun-protected areas** — *"bathing-suit" distribution on the lower trunk, thighs, and breasts; contrasts with sun-exposed sites favored by SCC in situ and BCC.*
- **Resistant to topical steroids** — *unlike eczema or psoriasis, MF patches persist or recur over months to years despite steroid trials.*
- **Stage-based progression** — *patch → plaque → tumor evolution differentiates from static benign or fixed inflammatory dermatoses.*
- **Hypopigmented variant in skin of color** — *pale, finely scaly patches without atrophy; helps differentiate from vitiligo (no scale, complete depigmentation).*
- **Variable color (red, violaceous, brown)** — *lesions may shift in color with stage and skin tone, requiring biopsy for confirmation.*

## Variation in skin tones

In skin of color, mycosis fungoides often appears as **hypopigmented papules and plaques without skin thinning**. Men with skin of color have presented with later-stage disease and more skin involvement than men with lighter skin tones, while women with skin of color have shown earlier-onset, more aggressive disease. In children, more than half present with the hypopigmented variant; only about 40% of pediatric cases show the classic adult appearance.

## Diagnosis

Diagnosing mycosis fungoides usually takes time and several biopsies. Early patches can look benign on histology — a single biopsy is often inconclusive, so multiple biopsies over months are common. Confirmed diagnosis shows infiltrates of malignant CD4+ T cells, with the cells preferentially invading the epidermis (epidermotropism). Workup includes immunohistochemistry, T-cell receptor gene clonality testing (PCR or FISH), and ISCL/EORTC clinical staging: **T1** (limited patches under 10% body surface area), **T2** (generalized patches/plaques over 10% BSA), **T3** (tumors), and **T4** (erythroderma — most of the skin involved).

## Treatment

Treatment depends on stage. **Skin-directed therapies** are first-line for early disease: topical steroids, topical chemotherapy (nitrogen mustard, carmustine), topical bexarotene (a retinoid), narrowband UVB phototherapy, PUVA (psoralen + UVA), localized radiotherapy, and total-skin electron beam therapy.

**Systemic therapies** for advanced disease include interferon-alfa, oral retinoids, bexarotene, denileukin diftitox, monoclonal antibodies, histone deacetylase inhibitors, and conventional chemotherapy. Only **brentuximab vedotin** (for tumors expressing CD30) and **allogeneic stem cell transplant** are considered able to change the long-term course of the disease — most other treatments control symptoms but don't cure it.

## Risk factors and prognosis

Survival depends mostly on age, skin stage, and whether the lymphoma has spread beyond the skin. Mycosis fungoides is typically slow-progressing — the estimated 5-year survival is around 87%. Roughly one-third of patients with limited (T1) disease eventually progress to advanced stages. Variant matters: **hypopigmented** and **poikiloderma-like** variants tend to have a better prognosis, while **folliculotropic** and **pustular** variants behave more aggressively.
