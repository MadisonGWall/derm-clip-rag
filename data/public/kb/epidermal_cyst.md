# Epidermal Cyst

## Overview

An epidermal cyst is a benign cyst that arises from the upper part (infundibulum) of a hair follicle and is lined by epidermis-like tissue. It fills with keratin and lipid-rich debris.

## Demographics

Epidermal cysts mostly affect adults in young-to-middle age and occur about twice as often in men as in women. They show up more frequently in patients with acromegaly and in association with Favre-Racouchot syndrome (a sun-damage condition).

## Causes

An epidermal cyst usually results from a blocked pilosebaceous unit (the hair-follicle/oil-gland complex). On non-hair-bearing areas — buttock, palm, sole — cysts can form when epidermal cells are pushed into the dermis by trauma, then accumulate keratin. Genetic disorders that increase risk include **Gardner syndrome**, pachyonychia congenita type 2, and basal cell nevus syndrome.

## Clinical features

Epidermal cysts present as a firm, flesh-colored or yellowish round papule or nodule. They're attached to the skin surface but usually move freely over deeper layers, range from 1–3 cm in diameter, and have a characteristic **central punctum** (a tiny visible follicular opening). Pressure can express foul-smelling cheesy debris — a mix of keratin and lipid — through the punctum.

They're most common on the central trunk and face but occur throughout the body. Rupture causes swelling, redness, and tenderness, often from secondary bacterial infection with **Staphylococcus aureus**, *E. coli*, or group A streptococcus.

## Key distinguishing features

- **Dome-shaped, dermal, freely mobile** — *cysts move under the skin and feel firm; epidermal lesions like seborrheic keratoses or warts are surface-level and adherent.*
- **Central punctum** — *the visible follicular opening is highly characteristic; absent in nevi, SK, BCC, and verruca.*
- **Expressible cheesy keratin** — *gentle pressure releases foul-smelling debris through the punctum, a finding unique to keratin-filled cysts.*
- **Smooth, flesh-colored or yellowish surface** — *contrasts with the rough or pigmented surfaces of warts, nevi, and SKs.*
- **Inflammation when ruptured** — *secondary erythema, fluctuance, and tenderness mimic an abscess but follow the cyst architecture, not bacterial infection alone.*

## Diagnosis

Epidermal cysts are usually diagnosed clinically. Biopsy is typically unnecessary unless the cyst is excised. Histology shows a single (unilocular) cyst cavity in the dermis, lined by stratified squamous epithelium without rete ridges, with keratohyaline granules in the lining cells. The main lookalikes are lipoma, trichilemmal (pilar) cyst, acne pseudocyst, myxoid pseudocyst, dermoid cyst, and HPV-related epidermal cysts.

## Treatment

Most small, uncomplicated cysts don't need treatment. The gold standard for definitive management is **complete surgical excision with the cyst capsule intact** — leaving any of the cyst lining behind raises recurrence risk. Infected cysts may need antibiotics and incision/drainage, though incision and drainage alone (without removal of the cyst wall) frequently leads to recurrence.

## Risk factors and associations

Recognized inherited associations include **Gardner syndrome** (multiple cysts with intestinal polyposis), **pachyonychia congenita type 2**, and **basal cell nevus syndrome**. Age (young-to-middle adulthood), male sex (about 2:1), and prior trauma increase risk. Squamous cell carcinoma very rarely arises within an epidermal cyst.
